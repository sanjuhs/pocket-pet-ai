# Pocket Pet AI: A Frozen-Model Personal Operating System

The product is essentially a private, wearable artificial intelligence companion that is always available, speaks naturally, remembers the user, understands parts of the surrounding world, and can control the user’s phone and computer. It is not simply another voice assistant like Siri or Google Assistant. The goal is to create something closer to a persistent digital creature or “pet brain”: one personality, one voice, one model, one set of abilities, and one physical device designed around that exact model. The model’s main weights are trained and finalized before manufacturing and are never updated afterward. Because the model never changes, its complete architecture, weight layout, quantization pattern, routing structure, super-weight locations, audio pipeline, vision pipeline, and decoding system can all be optimized together and physically built into a custom chip.

The final consumer product consists of three main parts. The first part is a lightweight ear-worn device, such as an open-ear earbud, ear clip, small headset, or futuristic headphone. This wearable contains microphones, speakers, basic audio processing, Bluetooth or another low-latency wireless connection, and possibly a small camera or connection to smart glasses. It is designed to be worn for long periods while allowing the user to hear the surrounding environment. The second part is the pocket compute puck. This is the real brain of the system. It sits inside the user’s pocket, clips to clothing, attaches to a belt, or fits inside a small bag. It contains the frozen-model inference chip, dynamic memory, battery, wireless radios, storage, power-management hardware, and cooling system. The third part is the companion software installed on the user’s phone and computers. This software gives the assistant controlled access to applications, notifications, files, calendars, messages, settings, browser tabs, and approved operating-system actions.

The ear wearable continuously listens in a low-power mode. Most of the time, the complete 7-billion or 9-billion-parameter model does not need to run at full speed. A tiny always-on audio processor performs voice activity detection, wake-word detection, noise cancellation, echo cancellation, speaker recognition, and basic acoustic-event detection. This low-power layer may consume approximately 0.2 to 0.8 watts, depending on the microphones and connection being used. When the user begins speaking or invokes the assistant, the pocket puck wakes the larger frozen omni model and starts processing the conversation.

The central intelligence is envisioned as a 7B–9B omni-modal model. “Omni” means that the system is not limited to text. The model can work with speech, text, images, camera frames, interface screenshots, tool results, and potentially environmental signals. It can listen to the user, interpret what was said, reason over the request, decide whether an action is required, speak a response, and control another device. Instead of connecting separate large speech-to-text, language, vision, and text-to-speech services through the cloud, the intention is to compress and optimize the complete multimodal pipeline so that it runs locally.

The model’s main language weights are trained at extremely low precision, ideally using a native ternary or approximately 1.58-bit weight system. Each ordinary weight can be represented as negative one, zero, or positive one. The theoretical information requirement for 9 billion ternary parameters is approximately 1.78 gigabytes. A straightforward physical representation using two bits for each ternary weight would require approximately 2.25 gigabytes. Once scale values, protected weights, normalization values, embeddings, routing information, audio modules, vision modules, speech-generation components, and other supporting parameters are included, a practical frozen model package would probably occupy approximately 2 to 4 gigabytes.

The important distinction is that these 2 to 4 gigabytes are not handled like a normal downloadable model file. Because the model is permanently frozen, the weights can be laid out inside mask ROM, embedded non-volatile memory, stacked memory dies, custom weight banks, or compute-near-memory structures. The chip does not need to support arbitrary future neural networks. It only needs to execute this exact model. Every matrix size, attention layer, feed-forward layer, activation scale, sparsity pattern, and execution order is known before the chip is manufactured.

The ordinary ternary weights do not require conventional multiplication. A positive-one weight means that the input activation is added, a negative-one weight means that it is subtracted, and a zero weight means that the operation is skipped. This allows much of the normal matrix-multiplication machinery to be replaced with highly optimized addition, subtraction, accumulation, routing, and skipping circuits. However, the model is not literally transformed into one enormous answer lookup table. The weights are fixed, but the input and activations remain dynamic. Every new word, sound, camera frame, notification, and user request produces new hidden states, attention outputs, logits, and memory entries. The chip is therefore still performing real neural-network inference, but it is doing so with a completely fixed and optimized physical dataflow.

The weights would probably use mixed precision rather than forcing every parameter into exactly the same representation. Most ordinary weights could remain ternary. Particularly important weights, including super weights, unusual outliers, normalization parameters, sensitive projection values, and some embedding or output-head values, could be stored at 4-bit, 8-bit, or occasionally 16-bit precision. A possible arrangement would place more than 98 or 99 percent of the model in ternary form while reserving a small protected pathway for the parameters that suffer unacceptable quality loss at extremely low precision. Since the locations of these important values are fixed, the chip can contain dedicated high-precision circuits only where they are required.

The main frozen language and multimodal weights occupy approximately 2 to 4GB, but the device also needs writable working memory. A sensible product would contain 8 to 16GB of fast LPDDR memory. A first premium or development version could contain 16 to 32GB, but a carefully optimized consumer version should probably work within 8 to 16GB. This dynamic memory holds the KV cache, current activations, temporary attention data, audio buffers, vision features, operating-system processes, user memory, tool state, and application-control information.

The most important variable memory is the key-value cache, or KV cache. This cache stores information derived from previous tokens so that the model does not have to recompute the complete conversation every time it generates another token. A conventional 9B transformer using a normal FP16 KV representation could require many gigabytes for a 120,000-token context. The product would therefore be trained from the beginning around a compressed latent-attention architecture rather than attempting to compress the KV cache only after training. A Multi-head Latent Attention-style system, grouped-query attention, low-precision KV storage, token eviction, attention sinks, sliding windows, sparse attention, and recurrent summary memory could all be combined.

Instead of storing complete keys and values for every head and every token, the model can store a smaller latent representation from which the required attention information is reconstructed. The most recent conversation may remain in a relatively high-quality hot cache, while older information is progressively compressed into lower-precision latent memory. Very old conversation can be converted into summaries, facts, events, embeddings, and relationship-state records rather than remaining as full token-by-token KV data.

A reasonable memory target would be tens of megabytes for a few thousand active tokens, approximately 64 to 256MB for a normal 32,000-token working context, and approximately 250MB to 1.2GB for an aggressively compressed 120,000-token latent context. The exact result depends on the model’s number of layers, hidden dimensions, latent KV size, precision, and attention architecture. The puck could therefore maintain a large conversation context without requiring 15 or 20GB of KV memory.

The user’s long-term memory is separate from the KV cache. The assistant does not need to keep every conversation permanently inside the active transformer context. It can store structured memories such as the user’s name, relationships, projects, preferences, deadlines, habits, places, previous decisions, recurring problems, emotional history, and important events. These memories can live in an encrypted local database on the puck or phone. When they become relevant, a retrieval system brings only the required information into the model’s working context. This creates the impression that the assistant remembers the user for months or years without continuously holding years of dialogue inside the KV cache.

Current activations are much less problematic than the model weights or long KV cache. During token generation, the chip can process one layer and then pass the resulting activations to the next layer. It does not need to preserve every intermediate activation permanently. A set of on-chip SRAM scratchpads, perhaps ranging from roughly 32 to 256MB depending on the architecture, can hold the hot activation vectors, feed-forward intermediate values, normalization buffers, and double-buffering areas. Attention scores can also be computed in tiles rather than storing an enormous full attention matrix. Each block is processed, combined into a running softmax result, and then discarded.

The output logits are also comparatively small. Even with a vocabulary containing tens of thousands or more than 100,000 possible tokens, the full logit vector only occupies a small amount of memory. The sampler can immediately reduce this to the most likely candidates using top-k, top-p, constrained decoding, grammar filtering, or tool-call rules. The audio buffers are similarly manageable. Raw 16kHz speech occupies only tens of kilobytes per second, and the device can use circular buffers rather than saving every sound permanently.

Vision requires more careful control. The assistant should not run full high-resolution video understanding at 30 or 60 frames per second continuously, because that would waste energy and memory. Instead, the wearable or phone camera can operate in an event-driven manner. The system might analyze one frame every few seconds during normal use, increase the frame rate when the user asks a visual question, crop only relevant regions, or track objects using a tiny vision model. Raw images are converted into compact visual tokens, object descriptions, scene graphs, or embeddings, after which the raw frame can be discarded unless the user explicitly wants it saved.

The speech system is integrated into the product rather than treated as an unrelated cloud service. The ear wearable captures audio and sends a clean low-latency stream to the puck. A dedicated audio DSP performs beamforming, echo cancellation, noise suppression, voice activity detection, and potentially speaker separation. An audio encoder converts speech into compact neural representations. These can be interpreted directly by an omni model or passed through a small speech-recognition component. The response can be generated as text, semantic speech tokens, acoustic tokens, or a hybrid representation. A small model-specific speech decoder and vocoder then produce the assistant’s voice.

Because the assistant always has the same voice and personality, its speech generator can be much more specialized than a general-purpose voice-cloning system. It does not need to generate every possible speaker identity. It only needs to produce one or a limited set of carefully designed voices. The voice can therefore be distilled and compressed substantially. The same applies to personality. The base personality is partly contained in the frozen model weights and system architecture, while the writable memory stores the evolving relationship with the particular user.

The decoding system does not have to rely entirely on conventional one-token-at-a-time autoregressive generation. The model could use speculative decoding, multi-token prediction heads, block decoding, diffusion-assisted decoding, or a two-tower structure in which one component maintains autoregressive context and another proposes multiple tokens in parallel. The custom chip can be designed specifically around the decoding architecture selected during training. This is important because ternary weights reduce the cost of each forward pass, but the assistant also needs to reduce the number of sequential forward passes required to generate natural speech quickly.

The target should be real-time conversational interaction. The user should be able to speak, briefly pause, and hear a response begin almost immediately. Perceived latency is often more important than the final peak token rate. A carefully optimized system might begin speaking before the full answer is complete by streaming generated text or acoustic tokens into the vocoder. The assistant can also use small acknowledgments such as “Okay,” “One moment,” or a natural conversational sound while the larger reasoning process continues locally.

The pocket puck is likely to operate at approximately 8 to 15 watts during normal voice conversation. This includes the frozen-model inference engine, dynamic memory, KV-cache reads and writes, audio processing, wireless communication, and system overhead. A lighter interaction may consume around 5 to 8 watts. Continuous vision, long-context prefill, complex planning, or high-speed generation could temporarily raise consumption to approximately 20 to 30 watts. The worst continuous visual and reasoning workloads might go higher, but the product should avoid operating in that mode for long periods.

The device could contain a battery in the range of 30 to 60 watt-hours. At an average active consumption of 10 watts, a 30Wh battery provides approximately three hours of use, while a 60Wh battery provides approximately six hours. If the assistant alternates between low-power listening and short bursts of conversation, practical all-day standby with several hours of active interaction may be possible. A removable or hot-swappable battery could extend the lifetime without shutting down the assistant. USB-C Power Delivery could provide charging and wired debugging.

Thermally, this is closer to a Magic Leap-style compute puck than to a normal wireless-earbud charging case. The enclosure would likely use a metallic top and frame to spread heat. It might contain a vapor chamber, graphite thermal sheets, carefully positioned vents, and possibly a tiny fan in a high-performance version. The puck could be approximately the size of a compact external SSD, thick power bank, or small rounded computer. A later chip generation could become smaller as the silicon, memory stacking, and cooling improve.

The ear wearable connects to the puck through Bluetooth for ordinary audio and control, but a higher-bandwidth or lower-latency wireless protocol may be useful for continuous multichannel audio or camera data. Wi-Fi Direct, ultra-wideband, or a proprietary low-latency radio could be used. The phone connects to the puck through Bluetooth and Wi-Fi. Bluetooth can carry basic control and presence information, while Wi-Fi handles larger data transfers, local model-tool communication, app screenshots, camera streams, and software updates for the non-model components.

Although the central model weights remain frozen, the device’s ordinary software does not necessarily have to remain frozen. The companion app, operating-system integrations, security patches, device drivers, tool definitions, and interface code can be updated without altering the neural model. The defining “personality brain” remains unchanged, but the adapters that allow it to interact with new applications can evolve.

Phone control works through a companion application that the user explicitly installs and grants permissions to. On Android, the app can use approved operating-system mechanisms such as accessibility services, notification access, media controls, intents, share sheets, app links, screen capture with permission, and automation APIs. The assistant can read visible interface structure, understand what is on the screen, identify buttons and fields, and perform permitted taps, typing, scrolling, navigation, and application launching.

The safest action pipeline is not simply “the model directly touches the phone.” Instead, the model produces a structured action proposal. For example, it may generate an action such as opening WhatsApp, choosing a contact, composing a message, and asking the user to approve before sending. A deterministic action controller verifies that the requested command is allowed, checks the user’s permission settings, executes it through the companion app, observes the result, and reports success or failure back to the model. Sensitive operations such as payments, deleting data, sending private messages, changing security settings, or sharing location should require explicit confirmation.

iOS is more restricted than Android. The product could still use App Intents, Shortcuts, notifications, share extensions, approved background capabilities, accessibility features, and direct integrations with cooperating applications. However, unrestricted control of arbitrary iOS screens would not generally be available in the same way. Therefore, the earliest fully capable product would likely work better with Android. An iPhone version would rely more heavily on app-specific integrations and approved user-triggered actions.

Computer control follows a similar architecture. A desktop companion application runs on macOS, Windows, or Linux. It provides the puck with a structured representation of open windows, selected text, files, notifications, browser tabs, accessibility elements, and application state. The assistant does not need to continuously stream raw screenshots if the operating system can provide accessible labels, button names, document structures, and window metadata. Screenshots or short screen recordings are used only when visual understanding is necessary.

The assistant can show information on the computer in several ways. It can display a small floating overlay, system-tray window, menu-bar interface, side panel, notification card, voice waveform, transcription panel, memory view, approval dialog, or full companion dashboard. A browser extension can display the assistant beside websites and allow it to read or operate on the current page with the user’s permission. A desktop app can show the ongoing conversation, planned actions, tool calls, files being used, device temperature, battery status, memory usage, and privacy controls.

For example, the user could say, “Find the latest invoice from my email, open it on my computer, and prepare a reply.” The ear wearable records the request. The puck interprets it and decides that it requires email and desktop access. The Gmail or mail integration retrieves the relevant metadata. The model identifies the correct invoice. The desktop companion opens the document and shows it on the screen. The model drafts a reply, but the deterministic controller presents the draft for confirmation before sending. The assistant then tells the user through the ear wearable that the document is open and the draft is ready.

The assistant could control ordinary computer applications through accessibility APIs, keyboard and mouse automation, browser extensions, local APIs, command-line tools, and integrations exposed by cooperating software. A hierarchy should be used. Native structured APIs are preferred because they are reliable. Accessibility-tree control is the second choice. Visual screen understanding and simulated clicking are fallbacks for applications without usable APIs. The assistant should observe the result after every action rather than assuming success.

Tool use is one of the most important parts of the system. The frozen model does not need to memorize every live fact. It can call tools for the calendar, contacts, email, messaging, maps, weather, files, browser search, smart-home devices, and phone settings. Tool definitions can be represented as fixed structured schemas. The model selects a tool, fills in the arguments, receives a structured result, and continues the conversation. The tool controller remains separate from the neural model so that security rules are deterministic and inspectable.

The device’s safety state is small. It does not require gigabytes of memory. It consists of user permissions, policy rules, authentication status, current device trust, action-risk classification, and confirmation requirements. The assistant can have a frozen safety prior inside the model, but actual permission enforcement should happen outside the model in ordinary verified software. Biometric confirmation from the phone, a voice PIN, a physical button on the puck, or a wearable gesture could authorize sensitive actions.

Privacy is one of the strongest product advantages. The main conversations, voice processing, model inference, personal memory, and ordinary action planning can happen locally. The puck can encrypt all writable storage, keep model memory separate from app permissions, and let the user inspect or delete stored memories. A hardware security module or secure enclave can protect encryption keys, authentication tokens, and private tool credentials. The user can choose whether any request is allowed to leave the device. The product can function without a cloud service for ordinary conversation and control, although optional cloud escalation could exist for rare workloads if the user explicitly enables it.

The complete memory arrangement could look approximately like this: 2 to 4GB of frozen model weights; 8 to 16GB of LPDDR working memory; 32 to 256MB of fast SRAM or on-chip scratch memory; 64 to 256MB for normal active KV context; up to roughly 250MB to 1.2GB for a 120,000-token compressed latent context; tens of megabytes for audio buffers and speech state; perhaps 50 to 500MB for temporary vision buffers and visual tokens depending on usage; and a relatively small amount of memory for tool state, action state, and safety state. The device could contain 32 to 128GB of encrypted flash storage for the operating system, long-term memories, logs, app data, offline knowledge, voice data, and optional photos or recordings.

The system’s logical flow is straightforward. The ear device listens. The audio DSP cleans the signal. The audio encoder converts speech into model-compatible features. The model understands the request and combines it with relevant recent KV context and retrieved long-term memories. It either produces a spoken response or generates a structured tool/action request. The action controller validates the request and sends it to the phone or computer companion. The companion executes the permitted action and returns the result. The model interprets the result, updates its local memory where appropriate, and speaks back to the user.

The physical product should feel less like carrying a computer and more like carrying a living personal operating system. The ear wearable is the voice and ears. The puck is the brain and memory. The phone and computer applications are its hands and eyes into existing digital systems. The frozen model provides continuity of personality. The writable memory allows the relationship to adapt without retraining the model. The tool layer gives it practical abilities. The permission controller prevents the neural model from having unrestricted access to sensitive systems.

The first proof-of-concept does not require manufacturing an ASIC. The initial demo can run the model on an existing GPU, desktop computer, high-end mobile SoC, embedded AI board, or compact mini-PC. An Android companion application can provide notifications, screen capture, accessibility control, microphone input, and tool execution. Bluetooth earbuds can provide the voice interface. A local server running the omni model can simulate the future puck. The demo should measure time to first spoken response, sustained token generation rate, speech latency, KV-memory growth, total RAM usage, average wattage, and action success rate.

The second stage would move the most important model operations onto an FPGA or configurable accelerator. This allows experimentation with ternary addition/subtraction units, fixed weight layouts, custom attention memory, latent KV compression, and multi-token decoding. The third stage would create a small inference ASIC or chiplet prototype. Only after the model, memory architecture, decoding method, and product behavior are stable would it make sense to perform a full custom tape-out.

The ultimate specification is therefore a wearable, local, real-time, multimodal personal companion built around a frozen 7B–9B low-bit model. The frozen model package would occupy roughly 2 to 4GB. The device would use approximately 8 to 16GB of dynamic memory and 32 to 128GB of encrypted storage. It would consume roughly 8 to 15 watts during normal conversation, with 20 to 30-watt bursts for heavy vision, long-context prefill, or complex reasoning. A 30 to 60Wh battery would provide roughly two to six hours of active operation, with much longer mixed standby time. It would connect to an open-ear wearable, phone, and computer through Bluetooth, Wi-Fi, USB-C, and local companion software. It would speak in real time, remember the user, understand selected visual context, display information through overlays and companion apps, and operate approved phone and computer functions through structured APIs, accessibility controls, and explicit permissions.

The final product is not merely a portable chatbot. It is a private computer operating system with a stable personality, embodied as a small wearable ecosystem: a voice in the ear, a frozen model brain in the pocket, long-term memory under the user’s control, and secure digital hands that can operate the phone and computer.
